About

Engineering programmable molecules into useful architectures.

Nucleic acids have well-defined helical geometries and predictable base-pairing rules. The Liu Lab applies these design principles to construct DNA and RNA systems that probe biological mechanisms and point toward new therapeutic strategies.

Led by Di Liu in the School of Molecular Sciences and the Biodesign Center for Molecular Design and Biomimetics, the group combines DNA/RNA nanotechnology, structural biology, cryo-EM, chemical biology, and translational thinking.

Research

Three connected ways of using nucleic acid programmability.

01

Synthetic DNA topology

We design DNA constructs with naturally inaccessible topologies to reveal how cells resolve topological challenges and to identify inhibitors of bacterial type IA topoisomerases.

  • DNA knots, links, and supercoiling-inspired systems
  • Topoisomerase mechanism and inhibition
  • Broad-spectrum antibiotic discovery concepts
02

RNA architectures for cryo-EM

We build nanoarchitectural strategies that overcome small size and flexibility in RNA cryo-EM, extending methods such as ROCK for high-resolution RNA structural determination.

  • RNA oligomerization-enabled cryo-EM
  • Functional and viral RNA structure
  • RNA-targeting therapeutic discovery
03

Programmable mRNA therapeutics

We explore how mRNA structure can improve intracellular stability, expression durability, and cell- or tissue-selective translation for vaccines and RNA medicines.

  • Structure-guided mRNA stability
  • Selective expression programs
  • RNA medicine design principles
